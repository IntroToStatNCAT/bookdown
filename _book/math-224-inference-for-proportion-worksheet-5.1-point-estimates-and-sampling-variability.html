Chapter 11 MATH 224: Inference for Proportion Worksheet (5.1 Point Estimates and Sampling Variability)

11.1 Basic Terms

  • Sample proportion \(\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}\)
  • Sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is the unbiased point estimator for the population proportion \(p\)
  • A value of \(\hat{p}\) is a point estimate
  • Error = \(\hat{p} - p\)

11.1.1 Central Limit Theorem (for Sampling Distribution of Sample Proportions)

When observations are independent (take random samples of fixed size \(n\) without replacement); the sample size \(n\) is large enough i.e. \(np \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p) \geq 10\); and sample size \(n < 10\%\) of the population size then the sample proportion \(\hat{p}\) is approximately normal with mean $ = p$ and standard deviation $ = $: \(\hat{p} \sim N(p, \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}})\)

That is \(z = \frac{\hat{p} - p}{\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}} \sim N(0, 1)\).

Notes: - The requirement that the sample size \(n < 10\%\) of population size is to have the standard deviation \(\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\). - If the sample size \(n < 10\%\) of population size then the \(\sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\) is overly estimated SD and the SD will typically adjust by a factor \(\sqrt{\frac{N-n}{N-1}} \times \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}\).

When using \(\hat{p}\) to estimate \(p\) the Standard Error of \(\hat{p}\) is the standard deviation of its sampling distribution: \(S.E._{\hat{p}} = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\)

When \(p\) is unknown \(\hat{p}\) is used to replace \(p\) then check \(np \geq 10\) and \(n(1-p) \geq 10\) (success and failure condition) estimated \(S.E. \approx \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\)

11.2 Exercises

11.2.1 Exercise 1

In a random sample with size \(n=9000\) the count of “yes” is \(x=250\). 1. Compute the sample proportion \(\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}\). 2. Compute the estimated standard error of the sample proportion \(S.E. \approx \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\).

In a random sample of 765 adults in the U.S., 322 say they could not cover $600 unexpected expense without borrowing money or going to debt. 1. What population is under consideration in the data set? 2. What parameter is being estimated? 3. Compute a point estimate for the parameter using the given information above? 4. What is the estimated standard error?

Compute the sample proportion and the Z-score. 4. What is the probability that at most 14 of selected 90 freshmen made the dean’s list? 5. What is the probability that between 14 to 20 students of selected 90 freshmen made the dean’s list?
